1.0 Executive Summary
Falcon Engineering is entering its next stage of growth. The firm's strong reputation, culture of integrity, and high technical standards provide a foundation that few engineering companies achieve. At the same time, Falcon has reached a scale where the current partner-led structure, varying operational recommendations, and uneven cross-group practices are beginning to limit consistency, growth, customer experience and partner bandwidth.
The purpose of this strategic plan is to strengthen Falcon's internal recommendations, clarify its leadership and organizational structure, and position the company for long-term, scalable growth - without adding unnecessary complexity or increasing partner workload. And, ultimately, make more money.
The plan is built around three integrated Operating Recommendations ("Recommendations"), each focused on the levers that create the greatest impact with the smallest day-to-day disruption. Collectively, the recommendations strengthen leadership readiness, align service delivery, and improve financial performance across the firm.
The Three Operating Recommendations (0-12 Months)
Recommendation 1: Leadership & Organizational Foundation
Falcon needs to formalize governance, standardize operations, and define its Mission, Vision, and Values (MVV) to support a future GM/COO and improve clarity across all groups. A key component of this recommendation is to choose who will lead the change and implementation until you're ready to hire a GM/COO.
12-Month Outcomes:
Clear governance and decision-rights model
Updated corporate structure (partner entry, exit, valuation, retirement)
Standardized SOPs and KPI definitions across all groups
Unified MVV communicated and embedded into hiring and reviews
Reduced partner time spent on administrative and alignment issues
Recommendation 2: Revenue & Market Alignment ($10M Revenue)
Falcon needs to strengthen revenue generation by clarifying all service offerings - for each group and product, repositioning the Energy group, and introducing a simple, predictable Business Development (BD) System.
Disclaimer: the successful tactical implementation of Recommendations 2 & 3 are built on the strength and timing of your ability to execute on Recommendation 1. Recommendations 2 & 3 can be implemented in isolation but will exacerbate the friction partners currently experience in day-to-day operations and in making strategic moves for growth.
12-Month Outcomes:
Consistent cross-group service messaging
Increased cross-sell and bundled service opportunities
"Energy 2.0" launched with clear services, pricing, and market positioning
BD system implemented with partner + PM involvement
Revenue path toward $10M becomes visible and predictable
Recommendation 3: Delivery & Margin Performance (20% PM)
Falcon will improve efficiency and profitability by strengthening scope discipline, accelerating early project execution, and standardizing PM and delivery practices.
12-Month Outcomes:
Reduced rework and scope creep
Faster early-phase execution  fewer late-phase inefficiencies
Improved utilization and forecasting
SOP-driven delivery rhythm across the firm
Consistent progress toward a 20%+ profit margin
Dashboard with relevant business and financial metrics

What is Required from Partners
One of the most important design principles of this plan is minimal partner bandwidth. Your time and effort are best directed toward billable hours. Spending too much time on tactical implementation puts present and future revenue at risk. You are the fuel for the profitability engine.
Partner involvement is limited to ~5% of time, focused on:
Approving key decisions (governance, MVV, Energy services, BD direction)
Participating in short workshops to increase alignment
Attending monthly scorecard and BD meetings (30-45 minutes)
Reviewing pilot results and providing directional input
PMs, Admin/Finance, and/or project lead carry the operational workload.

These are not signs of weakness. They are signs of readiness for the next stage.
Implementing these Recommendations now ensures Falcon continues to grow with confidence, alignment, and operational stability - while preserving the culture and partner autonomy that make Falcon successful today.

2.0 What We Learned
2.1 What's Working Across Falcon
Falcon has a strong operational foundation and a culture. Across partner interviews, staff surveys, and on-site observations, several strengths came through clearly:
1. High Technical Standards & Reputation
Falcon consistently delivers quality engineering work. Clients trust Falcon to fix problems created by others, which is a competitive differentiator in the region.
2. Strong Culture Rooted in Trust and Respect
Employees feel supported, take pride in their work, and speak highly of leadership. The culture is lived daily, not written on the wall.
3. Partners Who Care About the Work and the People
Partners are involved in the technical execution of the work, are accessible, and are accountable. This hands-on approach has built loyalty and stability.
4. Project Execution is Generally Reliable
Workflows from intake to delivery are familiar and consistent within each group.
5. Stable Market Presence
Falcon's long-standing relationships continue to generate steady inbound work. Even during market fluctuations, clients keep Falcon top-of-mind.
These strengths form the foundation of Falcon's next phase of growth.

2.3 The 7 Findings That Matter Most
After synthesizing hundreds of data points, interviews, survey insights, and operational observations, seven findings emerged as the most critical drivers of Falcon's next stage of growth. These findings directly shape the 3 Boulders in Section 3.
1. Leadership Bandwidth is Maxed
Partners are splitting their focus between technical work, people management, BD, administration, and strategic decisions. The current model is not scalable at $10M and beyond.
2. Operational Systems Vary Widely Across Groups
Mechanical, Electrical, and Energy operate as separate business units with different standards, expectations, and processes. This limits efficiency and increases friction.
3. Metrics and SOPs Are Not Standardized
Inconsistent tracking of utilization, realization, fee burn, and project health makes it difficult to manage performance or make data-driven decisions. Part of the challenge is limited use of Key Performance Indicators and over reliance on anecdotal project progress reports.
4. Energy Group Lacks Clear Positioning
Energy is strategically important but misunderstood internally and externally. Its service mix, pricing, differentiation, and market positioning require a structured reboot.
5. Cross-Group Collaboration is Underdeveloped
Partners and staff are not fully aware of what other groups do or when to involve them. This limits cross-selling, slows projects, and affects margin.
6. Business Development is Passive Rather than Proactive
Falcon relies heavily on inbound work and relationships. A predictable BD system is needed to support growth, stabilize revenue, and expand presence in targeted markets.
7. Margin is Lost in Coordination, Execution Timing, and Inconsistent Delivery
Most margin erosion occurs not because of technical issues, but because of:
poor scope definition
late or fragmented project execution
rework
inconsistent PM practices
unclear utilization expectations
These are solvable and will materially impact profit.
